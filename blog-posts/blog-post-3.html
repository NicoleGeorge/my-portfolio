A GUIDE FOR NEW LAW STUDENTS:
THE IMPORTANCE OF ACADEMIC INTEGRITY AT LAW SCHOOL Pt.1

July, 2019 by Nicole

This guide is designed to illustrate what academic misconduct entails, why it is critical to law students and what consequences are involved if a breach academic integrity is found. As first year law students, it is your responsibility to familiarise yourself with the Flinders University’s Academic Integrity Policy. It is our role as an institution, however, to impress upon you the importance of such inquiry and its impact to your future careers as lawyers. As the great Theodore Roosevelt once said; “to educate a person in mind and not in morals is to educate a menace to society”.

WHY IS ACADEMIC INTEGRITY AN ISSUE FOR LAW STUDENTS?

Academic integrity is at the heart of all higher learning institutions. Ideas, thoughts and research are valued in high esteem due to the vested commitment by those who have dedicated their lives to the betterment of society through research, intellectual inquiry and education. At tertiary level, academic integrity involves correctly attributing sources used in formulating arguments, research undertaken is recorded with accuracy and above all, honesty in claiming your work as your own. If one violates our policy, they will have engaged in academic misconduct, which is a serious breach of university ethos not just policy.

To clarify, academic misconduct involves:

anything that gives a student an unearned advantage over another. It includes the following; purchasing an essay; plagiarising paragraphs or whole texts; impersonating another to take a test; sneaking a speak at another student’s answers; smuggling notes into a test; padding a bibliography; fudging lab results; collaborating on an assignment when the professor asks for the individual work; or asking for an extension by citing a bogus excuse.

For law students, correct referencing holds particular weight in the eyes of admitting bodies and the courts. It displays a student’s ability to cite their sources when examining thoughts and ideas so as to verify their arguments and the ability to honestly attest to those original sources when attribution call for it .

On completion of your studies, in accordance with the Legal Practitioners Act 1981 (SA) , you will be applying to The Board of Admissions to obtain a license to practice law. Reasons pertaining the importance of academic integrity to law students are found in two key sections of the admissions requirements;

  • (a) that he or she is a fit and proper person to practice the profession of law
  • (b) that he or she has complied with the rules of the Supreme Court.

The manner in which disclosures are made concerning to the above conditions, illustrates an applicants’ suitability for legal practice in the eyes of the courts. From a moral perspective, the bedrock of the legal profession is built upon the premise that 'Legal practitioners must exhibit a degree of integrity which engenders in the Court and in clients unquestioning confidence in the completely honest discharge of their professional commitments'. Therefore, when a student displays a lack of academic integrity, it extends itself to more than a mere case of misattribution of a source, instead illustrating their lack of “a ‘commitment, even in the face of adversity, to five fundamental vales: honesty, trust, fairness, respect, and responsibility’”. Thus, contravening the root of academic integrity itself, instead displaying elements of both “deceit and dishonesty” . It is imperative that law students understand that ‘failure to live up to expectations at university thus raises serious questions as to a person’s capacity for the similar dedication demanded of legal professionals’. The fundamental understanding of this bridge between a your academic endeavours and professional legal careers is what academic integrity holds at its core.

BURDEN OF PROOF: ACADEMIC MISCONDUCT & THE LEGAL PROFESSION

While the notion of cheating is a widely accepted immoral act, unfortunately plagiarism is not understood to be held to the same standards by all tertiary students . The courts, however, do not share this view. As rudimentary as the concepts of cheating in an academic setting, Kelly J attested during the Re Liveri legal admissions case, that cheating in an academic setting is not something that students needed to be governed by, for it was a universally sanctioned norm for legal professional to seek unauthorised assistance in their pursuit of an academic qualification .

key examples

The following cases have been selected to demonstrate academic misconduct, as they offer a cross-section of incidents committed by students while studying law at an Australian university. Miss Liveri’s admission was rejected due to 3 serious counts of plagiarism while attending James Cook University (JCU) . Plagiarism involves a person claiming the thoughts and ideas of another as their own due to either incorrect citation or intentional misattribution. During court proceedings, the applicant’s ‘lack of insight into the nature and gravity of the findings against her’ , notably Miss Liveri’s comments that JCU were ‘levelling’ charges against her showed a aura of negligence in taking responsibility for her transgression. Ultimately, it was the applicant’s lack of contrition that contributed to the applicant’s admission being rejected.

Secondly, in the case in Re OG (A Lawyer), collusion and dishonesty which lead to his admittance to the legal profession, that were the central points of contention for the courts. Collusion involves two or more students, either discussing content of an assignment or sharing answers to an assignment, where the parameters of the task are clearly stipulated as being that of individual work as opposed to a collaborative effort. During hearings in Re OG, it was revealed that he did not make adequate disclosures, namely attesting to another’s actions which were found to be false, during his application for admission, resulted in him being struck off the roll. Further, Warren CJ stated that “his actions, therefore were the antithesis of a ‘realisation…of his obligation of candour to the court in which he desire[s] to serve as an agent of justice”. Throughout the process, OG continued to deflect his responsibility away from himself and onto his then classmate. The misinformation entangled within his disclosures, along with lack of candour during proceedings were strong factors in Warren CJ’s decision.

In our final case, Miss Joy Onyeledo, the Applicant, contested serious charges against her relating to plagiarism. Her first application for admission was rejected by the Supreme Court of The Northern Territory . The Applicant did not disclosure her misconduct in its entirety within the application nor during initial proceedings. This brought her character into question and doubt was raised as to her ability to uphold the ethical obligations needed in the legal profession. The Applicant’s second application was successful after satisfying the courts of her fitness to practice. The Applicant achieved this by offering further disclosures to the court, whilst these may not have been integral to proceedings, her openness clearly demonstrated to the courts that she now understood the seriousness of her misconduct and made headways in rectifying her previous conduct.

During the final judgement, Kelly J reasoned:

The applicant had not intentionally passed off someone else’s work as her own but that the ‘academic dishonesty’ she had been found guilty of by the University was a result of poor essay writing and referencing skills. Therefore, the major concern was whether the Applicant sufficiently understood her ethical obligations of candour to the court and I have concluded that she does.
