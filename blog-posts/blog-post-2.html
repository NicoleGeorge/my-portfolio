Cutrone's New Revolution:
A Theoretical Perspective

August, 2018 by Nicole

The Kelly Cutrone Millennial Leadership at People's Revolution. Is this a movement steeped in theory or is she paving the way in a new field of management? The fearless leader of the fashion PR firm People’s Revolution is second to none.

A self-confessed role model, teacher and coach to her Millennial employees is refreshing, particularly in an industry that is renowned for a no holds barred approach to gettin’ business done, as Cutrone points out, “if anyone really wanted to change the world, they'd bring in the fashion b***hes”. With this attitude in mind when discussing the dynamics within the office space that is People's Revolution, Cutrone professes: “I think that my office is a research and development laboratory for the ultimate power chicks. It’s tribal and individual”....'nough said.

On the surface, in an industry that is seen to be only skin deep, it could be assumed that Cutrone adopts a hierarchical organisational structure, where everybody knows their job…and their place. You could say that Cutrone’s style could be likened to Max Weber’s Bureaucratic theory of management, but that’s for another time...sometimes I have to tame the Geek Beast (but check out Farazmand, 2010, p.246 if you are interested...now get back in that cage). Testament to this assumption, her supervision of interns has been well documented on the now-defunct MTV reality show The City, as well as in her first novel If You Have to Cry Go Outside: and Other Things Your Mother Never Told You (...and between you and me, it's one freakin’ amazing read).

These elements, however, cannot simply be reduced to a bureaucratic aristocracy, as an organisational discipline is seen to be contingent on the individual employees themselves, as opposed to one rudimentary management style. Then how is it that one can be at the helm of such a dynamic and intense organisation without ruling with an iron first? Well, it is obvious that each employee is different, therefore should be dealt with individually, a concept that Cutrone understands implicitly:

“I don’t treat any of my employees the same. Each come[s] with a different skill set and they each come with character defects”

Therefore, the dominant managerial style can be present within a manager who advocates employee autonomy, where its assertion is reliant on individual circumstances. Although there is not just one approach in managing employees, research by Morse and Lorsch (1970) been found that the best approach is dependent on the nature of the work to be done.

Particularly in the Fashion PR industry, where “highly uncertain tasks require more extensive problem solving, organisations are less formalised and emphasise self-control and member participation in decision making are more effective” (p.62), much to the testament of Kelly Cutrone's Millennial Leadership at People's Revolution. Further, newer research has shown it is important for organisations to tailor tasks, whereby a positive correlation exists between the fit and nature of the task to be completed (Morse and Lorsch, 1970)…Stephanie Pratt packing up a sample range anybody?...anybody?

These sentiments are the foundations for the modern approach to this dichotomy, outlined in Contingency Theory (the Geek Beast is out of the cage, I repeat, it's out of the cage!!). It purports that a connection does exist between task, organisation and people, therefore, “the appropriate pattern of an organisation is contingent on the nature of the work to be done and on the particular needs of the people involved“ (p. 62)...Cutrone attempting to manage Roxy in Season 2 of The City perhaps? Therefore, it can be said that Kelly Cutrone’s People's Revolution is the embodiment of this new school of thought, whereby a nurturing and collaborative manager can adapt to situations and individual employee needs, where sometimes a dominant style needs to be drawn upon.
